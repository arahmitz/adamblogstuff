TL;DR
#

If you’re there for the TL;DR part:

My main takeaways:

  • It’s more worth to go when you have some experience in gamedev, there’s not much for juniors sadly
  • Find a clear goal for the GDC
  • Be proactive - reach out to people you want to meet
  • Problem-solving matters more than knowing everything
  • Conferences reward effort, not attendance
  • Find a time to chill out
  • USA is an awesome country for sightseeing
  • DRINK WATER (SERIOUSLY!)

If I were to go next time I’d:

  • Prioritize roundtables and workshops
  • Attend talks I am 100% sure I want to go for and spend more time with people
  • Seek depth over breadth in connectons

If you’re wondering if you should go, then GDC is most valuable if you:

  • Know what you want
  • Have your experiences to share
  • Are ready to engage actively If you’re very junior, other opportunities might be better, but if you have some experience under your belt and you’re seeking mentorship, growth or niche connectons - it’s absolutely worth it.

Aaaand if you’re interested how I got these takeaways, let’s go:

Three times a charm
#

It took me three consecutive years to get to the Game Developers Conference.

I’ve applied for the Polish scholarship in 2023 - for my first year in professional game development - I didn’t get in.

Tried again in 2024 - same result.

After applying at the end of 2025, on the 9th January of 2026 I got this email:

I cannot fully describe what I felt when I saw it in my inbox. Relief, excitement, disbelief, stress - everything all at once.

I was going to be a part of the 2026 GDC Festival of Gaming Polish Delegation.

Ever since I became interested in game developement professionally, GDC felt like the place to be.

I’d seen many talks from previous years (some happened 20 years ago like Character Rigging Best Practices by S. Theodore and D. Wilcox), heard about it from teachers, read discussions online - it kept coming up over and over again. GDC wasn’t just another conference. It was a real milestone. The kind of place where one gets to learn from the best, to meet developers from all over the world, to meet your heroes and maybe, one day, stand on the stage yourself.

That’s why, for me, this email mattered so much. It meant my first trip outside of Europe, first experience of the New World, a full week in a completely new environment, and most importantly - a chance to attend the biggest gamedev conference in the world. I knew this was going to be a once in a lifetime adventure and I knew that this is going to be a big step up in my career. I’ve started my preparations right away.

Of course, none of this would been possible without the scholarship founded by Game Industry Conference, Indie Games Poland Foundation and Creative Industry Institute.

I’m genuinely grateful for the opportunity - they’re the reason why this was possible.

First Impressions of the New World
#

Flying halfway across the world is a weird mix of exctiement and exhaustion. Sixteen(!) hours of travel later (with no sleep, to fix my jetlag right away), I stumbled out of the airport and thought:

This is actually my first time in a different country, where I can read all the signs.

I know it sounds small, but at that moment it felt really huge. I was really drained, but super excited that I’m realy there. San Francisco itself felt almost unreal - like a city I’ve only ever explored in movies, pictures, or games.

It sounds pretty cliché to say that USA is totally different from Europe, but really - it feels totally different! The architecture, the views, the weather, the sounds, the smells, the people - everything is just new. Especially the weather - it was around 9°C in Poland and over 20°C in SF during the day.

Wandering the City
#

Before the conference itself started, I was lucky to spend three days walking through the city - and I mean it, as I had over 25,000 steps in a day each day! As I wanted to get as much from this trip as possible.

One of my first Points of Interest was of course the SF China Town - the biggest one in the entire country. I immedietly was blown away by how big it actually was - it’s not like one or two streets, it’s literally a small town.

I was really blown away by all the brutalist architecure and little pieces of things I’ve only seen in games like the Transamerica Pyramid (my favourite “Parachute Tower” from GTA) or whole Fisherman’s Wharf that looked straight out of Tony Hawk’s Pro Skater series. Of course I had to see the Golden Gate Bridge - which is located in a beautiful National Park and the Lucasfilm campus that is nearby!

There are many things that surprised me while exploring the city - I highly recommend going to the Alcatraz - as the history there, especially the Occupation of Alcatraz, hit me harder than I expected. If you’re an art history nerd like I am - SF MoMA is also a a must see - especially for the DuChamp’s Fountain (1917).

It was pretty exhausting pre-conference - but I felt like every step felt like I was collecting a beautiful story.

The First Day
#

The First talk I’ve went to was Matt Oztalay’s “Hitting the 60 FPS in UE5 and Keeping It There.” - you know, your standard conference with the Epic guy known for conferences. It was nothing short of awesome - like his talks I’ve watched on Unreal Fests always are, but the real conference magic happened afterward.

I knew I had to go all in when it comes to networking - as this was my shot and I can’t risk being afraid of talking to people, so I just… scheduled meeting with every technical animator/character TD I could find in the GDC app. To my surprise - most of them actually answered me and agreed to meet me! The first meeting I’ve had was with Matthew Lake - whose book, Technical Animation in Video Games guided me through all the basics of Technical Animation - I was gifted it for my first year as an Animator in the industry after I decided I want to specialize in TechAnim.

Basically we went to grab a drink and nerd out about animation pipelines, tools, automations, challanges, and tech anim communities and suddenly I was invited to join a group of senior TechAnims in a cafe discussing real production challanges. Just like that, Matt introduced me to so many awesome people, who were all super welcome to talk about all things technical animation - I am really grateful for this opportunity, as I rarely get to talk to other people from my own specialization at home.

Later that day, I’ve got to actually meet all my animation heroes - from my mentor from Rigging Dojo, even to Harvey Newman - which some of you might know from his awesome youtube channel. I won’t lie - I was a little star struck. But the conversation quickly turned to substance: shared passion for the animation, lots of practical advice and encouragement.

Meeting all those people made me realise one thing:

I am not a just a visitor. I belonged.

The Real Goal of My GDC
#

If I had to give one piece of advice to anyone preparing for GDC, it would be this:

Find a clear, measurable goal

It’s very easy to get overwhelmed - the scale of the conference, the number of people, the number of talks. You really need to know what you’re looking for or you’ll end up just drifting through it.

I went to GDC with one goal in mind: find mentorship opportunities.

I wasn’t looking for recruiters. I wasn’t looking to sell my services. I wasn’t hunting for a new job.

I was looking for perspective - from people who are already where I strive to be.

So I did something really simple - but outside my comfort zone.

I messaged anyone, I could find whose job description included technical animation, rigging and tools development.

Really, everyone. I was very direct about it:

Hey, I’m moving jobs and transitioning into a proper mid-level role. I’m looking for senior staff for guidance and perspective on how to grow into a senior Technical Animator.

Coming from my experience, I didn’t expect many replies - in Poland I didn’t have a chance to get any really, but most people said yes.

It wasn’t just yes - they were happy to help, open, curous and willing to meet me and share their experience.

Looking back, this was one of the most important decisions I made for the entire trip.

Those meetings gave me something I couldn’t find in any video, talk, book or even at local conferences: real stories, real struggles, and real paths through the industry.

There’s one thing that stuck out to me: most of these people didn’t have a clear, guided path either. They figured things along the way - following their curiosity and their team’s needs - just like I’m doing now.

This realization helped me a lot. I was always worried that my AA-based career where I’m learning things by myself is a roundabout way in this career, but seems like even AAA based developers experience this. It made my whole journey much less chaotic and more… normal?

A thing I think about a lot post-GDC is how someone said:

You know, it takes a village to raise a Technical Animator. Or you know - an online community.

and it stuck with me. You rarely have a chance to learn a hybrid role like this from one mentor, one course or even one source. It’s knowledge you have to build piece by piece - from conversations, communities, tons of shared code, and other people’s experience.

That’s why my most important takeway is this:

Most people are happy to share their knowledge - if you’re willing to actually talk to them and ask.

And one more thing I always felt, but finally confirmed:

TechAnims are awesome. And they all love to nerd out about their work.
And honestly? that’s the best part.

Besides that, there are more things I’ve learned:

Think in Terms of Pipelines
Like really - automate everything you can. From validating files before you start your work, through every annoying click you might be doing in your work, up to the exporting process. EVERYTHING. IS. A. PIPELINE. It seems kinda obvious when you think about it, but really hits you when you’re going to actually support a team instead of working by yourself. Catching any possible error early is as important as the animations themselves.

You Don’t Have to Know Everything
Every Technical Animator I’ve met had a different focus - some were engine only, some were rigging focused, other were data wranglers or pipeline speciallist - or combination of any or all of them.

But there was a consistent pattern between these meetings: nobody knows everything. Everyone learns on the job ad hoc. This reinforces something I’ve learned at Rigging Dojo: it’s not about knowing it all, it’s about having the problem solving muscle and flexing it as much as possible - the real TD mindset.

My favourite part - Technical Art Roundtable
#

There’s an important thing to know about me when it comes to conferences - I. LOVE. ROUNDTABLES. Like really, they are by far my favorite type of conference activity. They aren’t recorded, so people feel like they can speak their mind in there. They’re open to discussion - but much more open than a Panel. I also love to talk with people - so it was only natural to go to every interesting roundtable at GDC to see how it’s done overseas.

Technical Art Roundtable is going the be my biggest highlight of whole conference, really. It’s been running for TWENTY YEARS at this point - Jeff, the moderator, started it at GDC in 2006. It’s really mindblowing. He also got an award from the community for doing it for all this time - it was awesome!

One of the biggest differences between this roundtable and all roundtables I’ve experienced at Polish conferences was lack of the main topic, besides it being about TechArt of course. Instead, attendees who got into the room early could write down topics they want to talk about on a flipchart and it was then used by the moderator to guide the conversation. As any roundtable - anyone can jump in or add another topic.

The result? Very dynamic, inclusive discussions that evolve over the THREE! days of roundtables. If we had no time left, the topic was continued the next day - for three hours in total of talking all things tech art!

During these roundtables I actually realized:

I’m no longer an animator doing technical stuff. I’m a Technical Artist with an animation focus.

Technical Art is an umbrella term - I’ve met many people on this roundtables talking about their pipelines, tools and especially - supporting teams. As I was listening about their daily challanges and aspirations i was thinking one thing:

I’ve found my people.
And I loved every second of it.

I’m actually going to try to bring this format of talking to Game Industry Conference back home: shorter sessions, maybe two smaller time slots and dynamic topics. I believe it could be an awesome addition for our growing animation community in Poland - stay tuned for that!

Reality Check
#

There’s one more thing I want to talk about when it comes to GDC that everyone who wants to go have to keep in mind. GDC Festival of Gaming is massive. Like… probably bigger than anything most of us experienced. There are pretty long queues for any talk, especially when it comes to known games (like Clair Obscure this year) or by famous people (coughs Rob Pardo coughs). You might not get into a talk unless you show up 30-40 minutes early.

I had to really adapt to that reality quickly - as the talks are recorded, I focused my times on roundtables (TechArt, Animation and Art Leadership mostly), I’ve tried to plan meeting gaps to be able to attend those, and used all possible downtime to recharge.

I’ve watched lots of videos about how to go about the conference and I believe there isn’t enough talk about how much you actually need to recharge during the event! GDC is a 5 day long conference - things are happening from 9am up to 6pm, then you have lots of different parties during the night. I’m an extrovert, I love to talk to people and be at parties but even I felt like my batteries were getting drained on all those official events.

I also feel like a big part of GDC happens outside of… GDC actually. The scholarship booked us a hostel in the city center, that felt like a real HOSTEL from a movie. The rooms were small - you could sleep comfortably, but there wasn’t much space to chill out, so everyone was forced to chill out together in a “common”/“chill” room. It was the best place to be, really. Everyone from the hostel (as we weren’t the only guests in the hostel) went there after all the parties to spend time together and I am really, really happy they did - as I’ve met many awesome people there.

The real strength of the conference is having great people around you - and I have to say I was really, really lucky about that.

Final Thoughts
#

When I applied for the scholarship in 2023, I felt like GDC is this distant, mythical event only the “best” ones get to attend, something reserved for the developers.

This conference is all about showing up, asking questions and being willing to put yourself out there. There are lots of awesome people to meet and it can be as good (or as bad) as you want it to be.

I went to GDC looking for mentorship, but I’ve came back with something more valuable:

  • a clearer direction
  • awesome connections
  • a lot of confidence in my own path and of course - an adventure that I’ll remember for the rest of my life!
